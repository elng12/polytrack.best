Mobile controls: Tuned for WR — sensitivity, deadzone, view

Mobile is not a downgrade. Tune sensitivity and deadzone, set camera and HUD for clarity, and adjust grip. With a few performance tips, inputs become steady and PBs repeatable.

1) Sensitivity, Dead Zones, and Turn-In Authority

Touch steering lives between fine control and decisive inputs. Too low sensitivity leads to constant micro‑corrections and lost speed; too high causes sharp, jerky fixes. Start mid‑range and run a “snake S” test: hold steady throttle through consecutive S turns, raise sensitivity until you hit apexes without extra corrections, then step down one notch for stable braking. That’s your baseline polytrack sensitivity.

A large polytrack deadzone delays turn‑in; too small causes accidental inputs. Size it just enough to filter bumps, not swallow the first touch. For tap buttons, prefer enlarging the active touch area rather than the deadzone — aim for quick activation with forgiving edges, not a high lift to trigger.

2) Visibility: Camera, Contrast, and Telegraphing

Small screens shorten your “read‑ahead” window. Tilt the camera slightly upward so more road ahead is visible; if FoV is adjustable, pick a slightly wider view to see entries earlier, and increase HUD contrast to offset smaller objects. Keep strong brightness or hue contrast between track edges and background for instant line reading.

Treat decor as signaling. Use consistent colors or shapes before hazards, with a “far cue + near cue” two‑layer structure. Move timers and HUD to the corners to reduce center occlusion — one of the simplest mobile polytrack performance tips for visibility.

3) Touch UX and Ergonomics

Place left/right steering near the thumb’s natural rest to avoid stretching to edges. Offset throttle and brake vertically to reduce occlusion at turn‑in. If you cover the line at entries, controls are too close to the ideal path.

Add tactile anchors: slight rimmed cases or grips improve consistency. Keep a neutral wrist; leaning forward shrinks thumb range. For long sessions, support the forearm to reduce micro‑shake.

4) A Mobile-First Practice Plan

Warm up two laps on a familiar track focusing on one skill: look early, single turn‑in, throttle like a dimmer. Then do five tries on a new map, note thumb overshoot spots, immediately retune sensitivity and touch areas; iterate while memory is fresh. Finish with one stable‑line lap to bank “controllable feel.”

5) Reduce Cognitive Load

Lower cognitive load, and fingers get faster. Reduce prompts once the route is known; if steering conflicts with camera‑pan gestures, disable one during races. Keep a consistent color cue system across maps to speed recognition.

6) Troubleshooting: Why Does My Lap Fall Apart?

  • Hitting inside walls: sensitivity too high at turn‑in; step down one notch and brake slightly earlier to stabilize inputs.
  • Late apexes: FoV too narrow or finger occlusion; tilt camera up and move controls away from the ideal line.
  • Sliding on exit: treat throttle as analog — feather first, then push; if needed, slightly increase deadzone.
  • Grip fatigue and shake: posture issue; support the elbow, reduce grip strength, and shorten session length.

Apply these settings and drills, and mobile can refresh PBs consistently. Continue with Ghost Racing to turn comfort and control into faster laps. With systematic polytrack mobile controls tuning, your stable laps will climb fast.
