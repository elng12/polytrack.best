Track Builder Bible: From idea to published custom track

This guide lays out a straightforward loop for Polytrack building: blueprint the run, tune the editor, manage imports, debug with data, publish safely, and keep a troubleshooting checklist. Follow the six steps below to turn sketches into repeatable, readable tracks.

  1. Blueprint: target lap time, difficulty curve, theme

    Pick a target lap time (short 30–40s, standard 50–70s) and write a difficulty curve that starts friendly, spikes in the mid-section, and rewards mastery on the finish. Choose a theme that drives materials and lighting, and document all constraints so every future edit references the same plan.

  2. Editor basics: camera, grid, and modules

    Calibrate camera angle and grid density until landmarks stay readable. Curate reusable modules, name them consistently, and prioritize line readability—use guard rails, contrasting textures, or lighting cues to telegraph turns before players arrive.

  3. Imports and version control

    Track the source, purpose, and version for every imported asset. Experiment on copies before touching the live build, and note any collision or lighting side effects together with rollback steps. A lightweight changelog prevents regressions.

  4. Debug loop: measure with “show last test path”

    Time every geometry change and compare ghost lines to the path overlay. Heavy steering corrections expose poor lines; wobbles after ramps hint at excess tilt or missing buffers. Log the issue → hypothesis → change → retest cycle until lap times stabilize.

  5. Publishing workflow

    Run a readability health check: two fresh testers should finish a lap without memorization, and at least one should improve across ten laps. Prepare a thumbnail that shows the racing line, describe the rhythm in one sentence, and publish with rollback notes.

  6. Troubleshooting FAQ

    • Looks great but drives poorly: cut decor density, boost line contrast, and widen sightlines.
    • Players miss entries: telegraph earlier with rails, arrows, or floor texture changes.
    • Ramp landings feel unstable: reduce tilt, extend buffer space, and retest with the path overlay.
    • Lap times fluctuate: flatten hidden bumps, align modules, and reinforce a single clean racing line.
